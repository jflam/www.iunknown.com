Dynamic language angst

I wonder if it's just me, or whether the community that I frequent has this on its collective consciousness, but I've been spending quite a bit of time wondering about the benefits of dynamically typed languages.


Sam Ruby pointed me to SWIG, which is a toolkit for building interfaces between C++ and various dynamically typed languages such as Python and Ruby.


Justin Rudd is wondering which is better: Ruby or Python? And he also offers up some opinions of his own.


Ted Neward participated in a panel discussion at the Rocky Mountain Software Symposium with Dave Thomas of Pragmatic Programmer / Programming Ruby fame, and offers a good analysis of the difference between loose coupling, loose binding and loose typing.


Personally, I spent some time at the bookstore the other day looking over Magnus Lie Hetland's Practical Python book. I really like the way the book is laid out, especially the way he offers up first and second implementations of the ten sample applications that he uses to round out his book.
