My other life
2001-12-01

I spent the years from 1986 through 1995 studying Organic Chemistry at the University of Toronto. Why the life change? In the middle of 1986, as I was preparing to graduate from high school, I was very unhappy with the state of software development. Remember, however, that I worked on Commodore computers, programming various incarnations of the venerable MOSTEK 6502 microprocessor. From 1979, when I first laid hands on a Commodore PET at George Brown College in Toronto, through 1986 nothing had changed. Sure, I had color graphics and 64K vs. 8K of RAM. Sure, I had a 1541 floppy disk drive vs. a tape drive. But that improvement wasn't even an order of magnitude. Contrast that with the change in PC technology from 1994 through 2001!

So, to protest the lame state of affairs in my little corner of computing, I decided to do something completely different. I decided to study chemistry at the University of Toronto. I had a lot of fun during those years. I accumulated my three degrees, and my mom is very happy that I'm a Doctor. I learned a lot of transferable life skills during those years as well: the ability to think critically, the ability to communicate both in writing and in the classroom, and the dangers of basing your work on untested assumptions.

During this time, I actually spent very little time working on computers. Sure, I used them to type up reports and perform calculations, but I spent very little time actually writing software. To be fair, towards the end of my Ph.D., I got the bug again and wrote some software to automate the pH-stat in our lab, and to analyze NMR spectra on PCs.

So, what did I actually do during my time in Chemistry? I worked in Professor Ronald Kluger's lab from 1990-1995, where I figured out the detailed mechanism of the fragmentation reaction of a model thiamin adduct: hydroxybenzyl thiamin. If you're interested in this stuff, check out my Ph.D Thesis.
