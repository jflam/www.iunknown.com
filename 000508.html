AspectJ makes me want to write Java code again

Gregor Kiczales is a master at teaching. He gave the final keynote at Middleware 2004, and oh what a keynote it was. Anyone who teaches for a living should study how Gregor delivers a lecture. He motivates AOP in a way that nobody else can. He motivates AOP better than Don Box can motivate COM. And that's saying a lot.

In the past, I had largely derided AOP as "programming by side effect". Recently, however, I'm not that sure anymore. AspectJ does such an excellent job at capturing cross-cutting concerns that I defy anyone to propose a better solution to those problems than AOP. It's true that it introduces a certain degree of ambiguity into the model - but the really nice thing about AOP is that it makes it really easy to remove the ambiguity after I've introduced it. That alone is worth the price of admission - the ability to quickly experiment with an existing code base. The AspectJ integration into the Eclipse IDE looks excellent BTW.
