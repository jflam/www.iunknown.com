Baby Boot Camp

Inspired by being at Fort Sill for a week, we decided to begin "baby boot camp" when I returned. Matthew's sleep habits have been, um, not so good shall we say - and recently they took a turn for the worse. Carolyn's been worn down to the bone - especially having to deal with my being sick and then away for a week.



p. On Saturday night we decided to let him cry it out. After reading from cover to cover Healthy Sleep Habits, Happy Child we watched for drowsiness signs, settled him down by a bit of rocking, put him into his crib, kissed him goodnight and walked out of the room. As expected, there was some protesting. But it only lasted 9 minutes before there was ... silence. After checking in on him and making sure he was sleeping soundly we declared victory - prematurely.

An hour later he awoke and protested for an hour and twenty minutes! That was really difficult to take, but eventually he settled down for another 3 hours. He awoke for two feeds - at 12am and 4am, protested 5 minutes and 30 minutes respectively before awaking at 7:00am - his normal wake-up time. He was his normal happy self in the morning, much to our relief!

Last night, he was even better. He slept from 8pm until almost 3am before he woke up to feed just once. After, he protested for what seemed like an eternity - another 40 minutes, but he clearly got more sleep on the second night than the first night.

Matthew's definitely learning how to fall asleep by himself, and he's also learning how to fall asleep after awaking in the middle of the night - we'll hear brief yelps occasionally, but they're becoming less frequent. With any luck, we should have a more rested family soon!

June 07, 2004

It's here!

My Inspiron 8600 showed up today. After tearing open the package, I discovered to my horror that there isn't a trackpoint device (aka eraser head). I guess I had assumed that Dells would have a dual pointing device, but I was wrong. Fortunately, I've adapted rather well to the touch pad (much to my surprise) and I'm not hating it quite as much as I thought I would. The only thing that I don't like is having to shift my hand to reach the touchpad. With the eraser, your index finger is positioned exactly where it needs to be without requiring reposition your hand.

I immediately repaved using Windows XP Pro, and got my baseline image (Office + Virtual PC) installed. I'm copying the April MSDN Library onto the host, and will re-install MSDN in my guest OS's.

The keyboard takes a bit of getting used to - it's a different layout from the Thinkpad. I miss the groupings of the function keys, and the Windows key is positioned exactly where the Alt key is on the Thinkpad. Overall, however, the action is pretty good - about what I had expected.

The display is incredible. I'm blown away by the amount of real estate that I now have. I'm still running fonts at 96ppi, and scaling them appropriately for applications that I care about (command prompts + TextPad + VS.NET). I don't mind having the menus etc. take up less space with the 96ppi fonts - I treat them almost like icons - they're not things you generally read.

One thing that I really like is the improved scrolling speed for ClearType-rendered fonts. My ThinkPad and it's crappy 8MB S3 graphics card was horrible at scrolling HTML help rendered using ClearType.

I like the fact that the USB 2.0 port can power my external hard drive. On my T23, I had to connect to a USB card AND the USB 1.1 port for power.

I'll have more impressions once I'm done installing software.

Second spindle

I picked up an external USB 2.0 drive today in anticipation of my new laptop arriving tomorrow. I did some quick-and-dirty comparisons of boot times for Windows XP running in a VPC session on my laptop from its internal HDD and the external USB 2.0 drive (a 60GB Fujitsu 5400rpm drive):

  • Internal = 75 +/- 1s
  • External = 50 +/- 1s

The +/- 1s is the standard deviation from 4 repeated measurements. That's a nice perf boost. I can't wait to see how things turn out on the Dell.

Fritz is an old-school writer

I'm amazed that Fritz Onion still writes using pen and paper!

My current writing system is fairly minimalist - I use a Textile-style simplified markup language (not paying that angle bracket tax!) that is processed through a set of Perl scripts that generate HTML documents. HTML will replace PowerPoint eventually, and my Perl scripts will eventually generate either PDF (via XSL:FO) or XAML. I like my writing system because I no longer worry about formatting when creating content.

It's about eliminating mental friction, which is why I think Fritz writes with pen and paper. However, I still like being able to get stream of consciousness thoughts into a document by typing.

Working as an IT Pro

As much as I hate it, there are times when I have to don my IT Pro hat. Earlier this week, Ian and Craig reported that they couldn't send email to my Exchange server. Ian was even kind enough to send me a detailed network trace of his Exchange server's attempts to communicate with mine.

A quick bit of poking around the event logs on my Exchange server quickly pointed out a potential problem: my secedit.sdb file was corrupt. Apparently this file contains Group Policy settings among other things. All attempts to repair this problem failed. What was strange about this problem is that some email made it through -email that wasn't sent from Exchange servers.

So I had to power up a decommissioned server, install Windows 2003 Server and Exchange Server 2003, configure it as a Active Directory Global Catalog Server, migrate my Exchange mailboxes, and reconfigure my firewall to point to the new Exchange Server. All told, maybe 5 hours of effort spread over 3 days. All because of one corrupt file. Wow.

A big thanks goes out to Ian and Craig for the heads up about the problem. Since my spam was still getting through, I assumed that all was well. This problem began nearly a month ago based on my server logs. I apologize if folks have tried to send me email and gotten bounced replies. If you're one of those folks, please re-send your message and I'll fast-track a reply back to you.

I'm scaring myself

I spent the bus ride into Toronto this morning reading the Root Kit chapter from Exploiting Software: How to Break Code:

This chapter's author (I'm assuming it's Greg Hoglund of rootkit.com fame) details some very scary exploits that compromise the EEPROM on various hardware devices (video, network, motherboard BIOS, HDD controller).

The more I think about these exploits, the more I'm afraid of the combination of root kits and "Layer 8" social engineering hacks. It would not be all that difficult for a well-funded organization (crime, foreign government) to place systems administrators into targets that install root kits for future attacks against the infrastructure of those targets. The potential havoc that can be wrought from these types of attacks is downright frightening.
