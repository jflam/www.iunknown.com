Random Walk Guide to Investing

Earlier I recommended a book called The Intelligent Asset Allocator. Today, I just finished a reading a far more approachable book that covers the same ground: The Random Walk Guide to Investing by Burton Malkiel:

I highly recommend reading this book before tackling Bernstein's book. It's a good mix of personal finance guidance with practical portfolio balancing advice. He also presents a much more simplified view of an investment portfolio than Bernstein's book. For example, in the equity portion of your portfolio, Malkiel advocates using extremely broad market index tracking funds like the Vanguard Total Stock Market Index fund that tracks the Wilshire 5,000 index. Contrast that with Bernstein's recommendation for a mix of large-cap, small-cap and foreign stocks (and that's his Level 1 (a.k.a. simplest) Asset Palette)!

Two books that I would recommend which suggest a different tack from the "diversify, diversify, diversify" asset allocation bromides are The Warren Buffet Way[1] and the classic The Intelligent Investor[2]. These two books (and the practice of the second-richest man in America, Warren Buffet) argue that value investing is the best way because you're looking at the fundamentals of your investments instead of just whether they're bonds, tech stocks, or foreign stocks. Asset allocation doesn't tell you much more than "you need 30% equity, 20% government securities, and 50% bonds if you're over 45 and have a short time horizon."

The problem is that diversification isn't about building your wealth as much as it's about protecting yourself from bad investments. If you do your homework and invest intelligently, there's no reason why you need such aggressive protection. In other words, it stems from a worldview that assumes that you're going to lose your shirt as opposed to one that says you can make a lot of money if you just do your homework.

Much of Warren Buffet's investment advice is also available in his annual letters as chairman of Berkshire Hathaway.[3]
