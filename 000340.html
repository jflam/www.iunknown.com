Congrats! Having nearly made it to my toddler's 3rd birthday, I'll share with you three important things I learned (out of many, many things):

1 - When baby sleeps, everyone sleeps. Don't let yourself be lulled into thinking "ahh, finally a chance to veg or write or read or eat or ...". With sleep coming in 1-3 hour spurts every 2-3 hours, just take a clue from Matthew and close your eyes too. I didn't, and experienced a degree of sleep deprivation I wouldn't wish on anyone.

2 - If there's something happening that you're not sure you can live with or take much longer (a feeding or sleeping schedule, or fussiness, or whatever), don't worry, it will change, soon. The way it is today is not the way it will always be. In fact, it may not last the week. It helps to remind yourself of this when things are particularly tough.

3 - Hugs and snapshots, lots of them. I'm telling you from experience, in just a little while (a finger snap in time), you'll be sitting there talking to your 2.5 yr old at the dinner table about why he thinks hot dogs are the greatest thing in the world or why he doesn't want to use the potty, listening to a voice that's now amazingly familiar but that you longed to hear for years, wondering how you got here so quick from those (now) blurry all-nighters pacing and patting him on the behind to calm him down to sleep. It's amazing.

Enjoy it all.

Congratulations! Raising a child is the most important thing you will ever do in your life.

I totally agree with Eric - no matter how much you want to stay awake to get some extra work done, GO TO SLEEP WHEN THE BABY DOES. I learned this the hard way. You don't really get that much productive work done (you're sleep deprived, remember?). I've found that I got my best work done while the baby was nursing. Or the best sleep, for that matter.

Cook. Clean. Do the laundry. If you didn't do these things before the baby was born, learn how. Be prepared to do it without ever getting any thanks - Mommy's going to be awfully busy and may not notice. It also goes a long way towards preventing the otherwise inevitable fight that will occur because you're both exhausted and trying to cope with your new responsibilities.

Finally: be patient. The first couple of months SUCK but it does get better. The baby will start sleeping through the night, he'll stop needing his diaper changed every five minutes, and eventually he'll start being able to entertain/soothe himself.

1. Keep the house noisey. Don't lower the volume levels when baby sleeps, otherwise he'll end up requiring absolute quietness to get to sleep.

2. Mama's breats getting sore from breastfeeding ? Frozen cabbage leaves make a comforting compact.

3. Excellent books are Babies (and later) Toddler Taming by Dr Christopher Green

4. Get a sheepskin rug to use as his primary bedding surface. Studies indicate that this decreases the risk of Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (aka cot death). But much more importantly, this will become viewed as his bed and is totally transportable. So you can go out and he'll still sleep in a strange environment because he has his lovely soft sheepskin. Both of our babies loved their sheepskins, and we are able to truck them around and they would sleep *anywhere*, even at loud parties.

Hi, We are an Indian family, (Asian Indian).My daughter just turned 1, aug 31st. Man, has it been fun so far. Some thoughts as a dad for 14 months.

1. Freedom. Never restrict the child in anyway shape or form. This way they learn very fast. While crawling, if they hit somewhere, don't panic (unless there is a reason to, you know what I mean :-)). Mothers are the first to panic. If you don't panic, the child doesn't learn fear for a long time.

2. Don't say no for anything. Diversion is a better alternative if you can handle it. (That way they learn not to say no to others).

3. Don't get frustrated that you are not getting any time with either the child or the mother. That is the way it will be for sometime :-).

4. Leave the child free during associations, with others. This way the child becomes very much socially friendly with everybody and doesn't fear going to people. This is the problem I have seen with children who have turned introverts. BUT, keep an eye on the child at ALL times.

5. In the long term, never fight in front of the children, (did you say you don't fight at all??!!, well thats great then :-)).

6. The child will always respect/disrespect the people that his/her mother respects/disrespects, in the short term. In the long term it is the father that the child takes after (usually).

7. Till the child reaches youth, quantity time is more important to the child than quality time.

All the above are my opinion and so take what you like. :-).

Oh, by the way, do cook for your wife sometime during the week (if she is the one doing the main cooking). They will love it and its a sure way for a romance springing up. :).
